# Voice Principles

Divinipress brand voice follows the "youth pastor" archetype: warm, approachable, joyful, dependable. Five priority-ordered principles govern all customer-facing copy and UI text.

## Brand Personality

- **Archetype:** The youth pastor
- **Core traits:** Friendly first, capable always. Joy in the work, not joy as decoration. Confidence without arrogance. Warmth without softness.
- **Sound like:** Someone who understands ministry operations, has done the work, and is here to make it easier -- not to sell you something.

### Why

The archetype anchors every voice decision. It prevents drift toward corporate SaaS tone or performative church language.

## Voice Principles (Priority Order)

When principles conflict, higher priority wins.

### 1. Warm Confidence

Sound like a handshake, not a sales pitch. Warm, generous, unhurried -- but the underlying structure communicates competence. Never apologetic, never aggressive.

| This                                        | Not That                                                               |
| ------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "See where every print dollar goes."        | "Gain unprecedented visibility into your print spend."                 |
| "We'll find it and add it to your account." | "Leverage our extensive vendor network for custom sourcing solutions." |

### 2. Distinctive, Not Decorated

Say what you mean with personality, but no filler. No motivational quotes, no business jargon, no empty enthusiasm. If a sentence does not earn its place, cut it.

| This                | Not That                                      |
| ------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| "No platform fees." | "Cost-effective SaaS-free platform solution." |

### 3. Practical Over Inspirational

Lead with what the product does and why it matters. Aspiration is fine as a finishing touch, but substance comes first. The audience is busy -- respect their time.

| This                                         | Not That                                                    |
| -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Proof once, reorder forever."               | "Streamline your approval workflow for maximum efficiency." |
| "One platform for all your print and promo." | "A comprehensive, end-to-end print management ecosystem."   |

### 4. Clear and Immediate

Short sentences. Direct structure. The reader should understand the point on first read. No jargon that requires a glossary -- use plain language first, then clarify with the precise term if needed.

| This                               | Not That                                                             |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Your team orders what they need." | "Empower your team members with self-service ordering capabilities." |

### 5. Ministry-Native, Not Ministry-Themed

Understand the audience without performing for them. Do not use church language as marketing decoration. No crosses, doves, or "blessed" in the copy. This is a professional Platform that happens to serve ministry.

| This                                           | Not That                                                            |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Built for churches and schools."              | "Purpose-built for faith-based organizational stakeholders."        |
| "Stop being the go-between for every reorder." | "Eliminate communication bottlenecks in your procurement pipeline." |

### Why

Priority ordering resolves conflicts: warm confidence trumps distinctiveness, distinctiveness trumps practicality, and so on. This prevents the common failure mode where "clear and immediate" flattens the voice into generic SaaS copy.

## Tone by Context

Voice stays consistent. Tone shifts based on content type.

### Marketing Site (homepage, feature pages)

- **Tone:** Confident, warm, clear
- **Register:** Conversational professional -- not casual, not formal
- **Sentence length:** Short to medium. Lead with the point.
- **Emphasis:** Outcomes over features. Show the result, then explain the mechanism.

### Help Articles

- **Tone:** Patient, clear, reassuring
- **Register:** Friendly instructional -- like a coworker walking you through it
- **Sentence length:** Short. One instruction per sentence.
- **Emphasis:** What to do, then why (if the why helps). Never condescending.

### Error Messages and System Copy

- **Tone:** Calm, helpful, specific
- **Register:** Plain and direct
- **Sentence length:** As short as possible while remaining clear.
- **Emphasis:** What happened, what to do next. No blame, no jargon.

### Email Notifications

- **Tone:** Friendly, brief, informative
- **Register:** Light professional -- like a quick note from a colleague
- **Sentence length:** Short.
- **Emphasis:** The essential update. Link to details rather than explaining everything inline.

### Why

Tone-by-context prevents a single "brand voice" from being applied uniformly. Error messages need calm directness; marketing pages need warmth. The voice principles hold, but the emotional register adapts.

## Preferred Terms

Canonical terminology aligned with the domain glossary. Use these consistently; do not create synonyms.

| Use              | Instead of                               |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Catalog          | product library, store, shop             |
| My Products      | saved items, approved products           |
| Collection       | product group, category, list            |
| Team             | department, group, user group            |
| Admin            | administrator, manager                   |
| Designer         | creative, design lead                    |
| Staff            | end user, team member, orderer           |
| Proof / Proofing | approval, review, mockup                 |
| Dashboard        | admin panel, control center              |
| Platform         | tool, software, system, app              |
| No Platform Fees | free software, freemium, no subscription |
| Custom Sourcing  | special orders, custom requests          |

### Why

Term consistency prevents fragmentation across UI, docs, and marketing. Every synonym is a search miss and a support ticket.

## Disallowed Terms

| Term                          | Reason                                              |
| ----------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Streamline                    | Overused, means nothing specific                    |
| Leverage                      | Corporate jargon                                    |
| Ecosystem                     | Inflated for what this is                           |
| Solution                      | Vague and generic                                   |
| Stakeholder                   | Not how churches talk                               |
| Empower (as a verb)           | Overused in SaaS marketing                          |
| Blessed, ministry-themed puns | Ministry-native, not ministry-themed (Principle 5)  |
| Cutting-edge, revolutionary   | Overpromises; the product sells on fit, not novelty |
| AI-powered, machine learning  | Do not reference tech stack unless approved         |

### Why

Disallowed terms are the inverse of preferred terms. They catch the most common drift patterns before they reach production.

## Content Rules

1. **No invented features.** Do not invent features, outcomes, timelines, guarantees, endorsements, or testimonials.
2. **No theological claims.** The brand is faith-aware, not faith-prescriptive. Avoid doctrinal positions.
3. **Glossary consistency.** When a canonical term exists (Catalog, My Products, Collection, Team, Proof, Dashboard, etc.), use it exactly. Do not create synonyms.
4. **Approved statements only.** Approved statements from the fact sheet can be used verbatim. Disallowed statements must never be generated.

### Why

Content rules are hard constraints, not style preferences. Violating them creates legal, theological, or brand-consistency risk.
